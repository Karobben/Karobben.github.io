Abstract

In plants, Long-chain acyl-CoA synthetases (LACSs) play key roles in activating fatty acids to fatty acyl-CoA thioesters, which are then further involved in lipid synthesis and fatty acid catabolism. LACSs have been intensively studied in Arabidopsis, but its evolutionary relationship in green plants is unexplored. In this study, we performed a comprehensive genome-wide analysis of the LACS gene family across green plants followed by phylogenetic clustering analysis, gene structure determination, detection of conserved motifs, gene expression in tissues and subcellular localization. Our results identified LACS genes in 122 plant species including algae, low land plants (i.e., mosses and lycophytes), monocots, and eudicots. In total, 697 sequences were identified, and 629 sequences were selected because of alignment and some duplication errors. The retrieved amino acid sequences ranged from 271 to 1056 residues and diversified in intron/exon patterns in different LACSs. Phylogenetic clustering grouped LACS gene family into six major clades with distinct potential functions. This classification is well supported by examining gene structure and conserved motifs. Also, gene expression analysis and subcellular localization substantiate with clade division in the phylogeny, indicating that the evolutionary pattern is visible in their functionality. Additionally, experimental analysis of lacs2 mutant validated that LACS2 plays key roles in suberin synthesis. Thus, our study not only provides an evolutionary mechanism underlying functional diversification but also lays the foundation for further elucidation of the LACS gene family.

Introduction

Long-chain acyl-CoA synthetases (LACSs) belong to the carboxyl-CoA ligase superfamily, which are also known as acyl-activating enzymes (Lu et al., 2009; Shockey and Browse, 2011). Most of the enzymes in this superfamily carry out a two-step mechanism. This two-step ping-pong reaction associates the transfer of adenylate from ATP to a fatty acid to form an enzyme-bound acyl-adenylate intermediate and pyrophosphate. Subsequently, this intermediate is attacked by a CoA to produce an acyl-CoA and an adenosine monophosphate (AMP) (Shockey and Browse, 2011). LACSs usually esterify free long-chain fatty acids (LCFAs) produced by de novo FA synthesis to form fatty acyl-CoA thioesters, which are subsequently channeled into many metabolic pathways for producing membrane lipids, storage lipids (triacylglycerols [TAGs]), surface lipids (wax, cutin and suberin) and fatty acid catabolism (Tonon et al., 2005). In addition, some LACSs are identified to also function as transporters of fatty acids between different subcellular compartments (Tonon et al., 2005; Jessen et al., 2015).

Arabidopsis contains one of the largest known LACS families with nine LACS genes, most of which were well characterized (Shockey et al., 2002; Zhao et al., 2019). LACS1, LACS2 and LACS4 were reported to be localized in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) and redundantly involved in the synthesis of surface wax and/or cutin (Lu et al., 2009; Zhao et al., 2010; Jessen et al., 2011, 2015). Among these three genes, LACS1 plays a major role in modifying very long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs, C20–C30) for wax synthesis since its mutation caused a strong wax deficient glossy phenotype (Lu et al., 2009), whereas a single mutation of either LACS2 or LACS4 only results in a slight reduction of wax components. LACS1 also involves in cutin synthesis by preferentially modifying C16:0 (Lu et al., 2009). In addition, LACS2 mutation caused strong cutin deficiency while it has little effects on wax biosynthesis under normal growth conditions, indicating that LACS2 is mainly involved in cutin biosynthesis (Schnurr et al., 2004; Lu et al., 2009). Double mutant analysis indicated that LACS1 and LACS2 have overlapping functions in both plant wax and cutin synthesis (Lu et al., 2009). Recent studies showed that LACS2 mutation also resulted in reduced wax accumulation under submergence growth conditions, which further confirmed that LACS2 is also involved in wax synthesis under stress conditions (Xie et al., 2020). Moreover, LACS4 in combination with LACS1 activates proper pollen coat formation (Jessen et al., 2011). LACS9 is the only LACS gene specifically localized in the outer membrane of the plastid envelope, which in combination with LACS4 was reported to be involved in the process of lipid flux from the ER to the plastid for glycerolipid synthesis (Jessen et al., 2015). lacs9 null mutant does not show any detectable phenotype (Schnurr et al., 2002), however, double mutant analysis indicates that LACS9 functions redundantly with either LACS1 or LACS4 in seed TAG biosynthesis (Zhao et al., 2010; Jessen et al., 2015). LACS8, another ER-specific form, also has redundant function with that of LACS4 and LACS9, since knockout of LACS8 in the lacs4 lacs9 double mutant background results in lethality (Jessen et al., 2015). Different from the above mentioned LACS members, both LACS6 and LACS7 located in peroxisome has an overlapping role in β-oxidation (Fulda et al., 2002, 2004). The overall function of LACS3 and LACS5 is not revealed till now. As mentioned above, similar to the function of LACS gene families characterized in other Eukaryotes species, Arabidopsis LACS genes not only vary in their subcellular locations, expression patterns, and substrates specificities but also present a complex network of redundant LACS activities involved in different aspects of lipid metabolism (Digel et al., 2009; Zhao et al., 2019).

Even though the LACS genes from Arabidopsis are well characterized, however, only a few LACS homologs from other plant species have been characterized. GhACS1, an orthologue of the Arabidopsis LACS4 or LACS5, is predominantly accumulated in the anther and plays an essential role in microsporogenesis in anther development of Cotton (Wang and Li, 2009). GmACSL2, a peroxisomal isoform of LACS from soybean, facilitates fatty acid and lipid degradation during seed germination (Yu et al., 2014). HaLACS1 and HaLACS2 of sunflower, with high sequence homology with LACS9 and LACS8 genes of Arabidopsis, are highly expressed in developing seeds and perform an essential role in oil synthesis of sunflower seeds (Aznar-Moreno et al., 2014), which is similar to the function of LACS9 in Arabidopsis. Unlike AtLACS2, which is specifically expressed in the epidermis for both cutin and wax synthesis, its orthologue in Brassica napus BnLACS2 is predominantly expressed in developing seeds and involved in seed oil production (Ding et al., 2020). LuLACS8A identified from flax (Linum usitatissimum) shows substrate preference towards α-linoleic acid and contributes to the biosynthesis of α-linolenic acid enriched in flaxseed oil cooperating with LuDGAT2 (Magwanga et al., 2018).

2. Materials and methods

2.1. LACS genes identification and their homologs in plants

Despite the above intriguing and significant functions, it is still necessary to explore the evolutionary relationship of LACS family since it can reveal existing functional data from different lineages and also provides some clues to explore the possible functions of those unknown LACS members in different plant species. Thus, we made a genome-wide comparative analysis, constructed a phylogenetic tree, and examined gene structure and gene expression profiling. These studies will address these questions: (1) How plants’ LACSs genes are classified in various clades based on a phylogenetic approach. (2) Is there any difference in gene structure, composition, and expression patterns of LACSs genes among various plants. (3) How different isoforms of LACSs genes are expressed at the subcellular level and in various tissues.

We performed an extensive literature survey to search LACSs genes and their isoforms that have already been reported and characterized in A. thaliana. Subsequently, we retrieved A. thaliana sequences keywords (LACS1 [AT2G47240], LACS2 [AT1G49430], LACS3 [AT1G64400], LACS4 [AT4G23850], LACS5 [AT4G11030], LACS6 [AT3G05970], LACS7 [AT5G27600], LACS8 [AT2G04350], and LACS9 [AT1G77590]) using Basic Local Alignment Tool (BLASTp and TBLASTx) searches in the Phytozome (www.phytozome.net) and GenomeNet (www.genome.jp/tools/blast) databases. The downloaded sequences were compiled into a single FASTA file. Protein classification was carried out by utilizing Pfam (Pfam.sanger.ac.uk/search) (Punta et al., 2011) and PANTHER (www.pantherdb.org/) (Mi et al., 2013) classification systems. In our database, all taxa were designated by four letters acronym, where the first letter represents the genus and the next three letters indicate the first three letters of the species, for example, A tha assigned to A. thaliana. These acronyms used in all analyses indicated with the accession numbers (e.g., A_tha_AT1G77590 corresponds to A. thaliana LACS9), but for phylogenetic tree analysis, we used the full name of species with their IDs. We provided a comprehensive description of accession numbers, protein length, protein domain presence, and intron number are provided in supplementary tables S1–S4.

2.2. Alignment and phylogeny inference

DNA and amino acid sequences were adjusted through MAFFT (Katoh and Toh, 2010) alignment accessible in CIPRES Science Gateway (CIPRES) (Miller et al., 2010). Changes were made manually in BioEdit v.7.0 (Hall et al., 2011), whereas ambiguously aligned positions were removed in the final analysis. To select the best model for protein evolution, Smart Model Selection (SMS) was used (Lefort et al., 2017), which showed JTT + I + G model for protein sequences based on the lowest BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) score (97,536) in PhyML (Guindon et al., 2010). JModelTest (Posada, 2008) was selected for nucleotide evolution, which revealed General Time Reversible model with the shape of Gamma distributions plus the proportions of variables sites (GTR + I + G) as the best fit model to reconstruct the phylogenetic relationship of nucleotides. We reconstruct ML phylogenetic tree using MrBayes 3.2.7 (Ronquist et al., 2012) in CIPRES with 1,000,000 generations with four Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and two runs. The reliability of the phylogenetic analysis was verified through the construction of an ML tree utilizing FastTree v2.1.8 (Price et al., 2010) with the best fit model. The tree was visualized and annotated in iTOL with default sorting (Letunic and Bork, 2019).

2.3. Gene structure analysis and conserved motif detection

Intron/exon pattern was determined using an online gene structure display server (Guo et al., 2007); gsds.cbi.pku.edu.cn). To retrieve gene structure in addition to the phylogenetic tree, a multi FASTA format sequence set based on genomic and CDS for LACS genes of selected species (which were common in all clades) was submitted to GSDraw. The MEME program (www.meme-suite.org/meme/tools/meme) was used to find significant functional motifs of LACS (Bailey et al., 2015). We adjusted the parameters as: distribution of motifs = any number of repetitions, the maximum number of motifs = 20, and the optimum motif width = 3 to 300 residues. The identified motifs were further analyzed by Pfam analysis for protein classification (Punta et al., 2011); Pfam.sanger.ac.uk/search).

2.4. Gene expression prediction and subcellular localization

To examine tissue specificity and expression intensity of representative species, GENEVESTIGATOR website (www.genevestigator.com/gv/) (Hruz et al., 2008) was used. The hierarchical clustering tool available in GENEVESTIGATOR was utilized to carry out this analysis. The highest expression values for genes containing more than one probe set were considered. Pearson coefficient was utilized to classify gene-wise expression data. We represented percent expression of various anatomical regions and developmental stages from LACS and putative LACS genes in heat maps.

To better understand and predict the subcellular localization of different LACS for various functions, we used an online website (bioinfo.usu.edu/Plant-mSubP) (Sahu et al., 2019). The full-length protein sequences were uploaded, and different approaches were used based on Amino Acid Composition (AAC), Dipeptide Composition (Dipep), Pseudo Amino Acid Composition (PseAAC), and N-Center-C terminal Composition (NCC). This approach successfully predicts 11 single locations (cell membrane, cell wall, plastid, cytoplasm, ER, extracellular, Golgi apparatus, mitochondrion, nucleus, peroxisome, and vacuole) and three significant multi-location proteins (cytoplasm-nucleus, mitochondrion-plastid, and cytoplasm-Golgi apparatus).

2.5. Suberin analysis

Roots of 10-day-old seedlings of lacs2-3 (GABI-Kat line 368C02) and Col-0 grown on 1/2 MS medium agar plates were harvested and immediately immersed in hot isopropanol at 80 °C for 10 min. After cooling to room temperature, samples were stirred for 1–2 h. Then centrifuged the samples for 5 min at 2000 rpm and discarded the isopropanol. Added the same volume of isopropanol and stirred for the whole day. For delipidation, the isopropanol was then replaced by CHCl3/CH3OH (2/1 v/v), CHCl3/CH3OH (1/2 v/v), and CH3OH, respectively, and stirred the whole day. Then, centrifuged the samples for 10 min at 2000 rpm, removed solvent, and dried it under a hood overnight. The samples were finally dried in a vacuum desiccator for two days. Dried tissues were then incubated in 5 ml of depolymerization solution (0.9 ml methyl acetate, 1.5 ml sodium methoxide, and 3.6 ml methanol) at 60°C for 2 h. 25 μg methyl heptadecanoate was used as an internal standard. After cooling, the fatty acid methyl esters were extracted by adding methylene dichloride (10 ml) and (0.5 ml) glacial acetic acid to pH 4–5 to the depolymerized samples. The organic layer was washed three times with 10 ml saline solution (0.5 M NaCl), dried with anhydrous sodium sulfate (6 g), and then evaporated under nitrogen gas. Monomers were derivatized in 100 μl of pyridine and 100 μl of acetic anhydride at 60 °C for 1 h. After cooling, evaporated under nitrogen at room temperature and redissolved in 100 μl heptane:toluene (1:1, v/v). The composition and amount of cutin monomers were analyzed by Agilent 8860 series GC equipped with an FID and a 30 m, 0.25 mm internal diameter DB-5 capillary column, with helium as the carrier gas. The GC was programmed with an initial temperature of 80 °C and increased at 15 °C min−1 to 200 °C, then increased at 2 °C min−1 to 300 °C, maintained for 10 min at 300 °C. Quantification was based on uncorrected FID peak areas relative to the internal standard methyl heptadecanoate peak area.

3. Results

3.1. Genome-wide identification of LACS homologs sequences in plants

Nine genes are annotated as LACS in Arabidopsis thaliana. Amino acid sequences encoding by these genes were used as queries. To search LACS homologs genes, a broad survey of fully sequenced genomes was carried out in total 122 plant species, which are obtained from two websites (www.genome.jp/(64 plants) and phytozome.jgi.doe.gov/pz/portal.html (106 plants) (Table S2). The whole data set consists of Rhodophyta (2), Chlorophyta (13), Embryophyta (3), Tracheophyte (1), and Angiosperm (105). BLASTp and BLASTx were performed against these LACS gene and their putative proteins. Searching BLASTp, multiple hits were selected for all plants. Although the range is indeed 271–1056, most (97%) of sequences have between 608 and 737 amino acids. The latter range is highly consistent for LACS proteins from numerous non-plant species. We identified a total of 697 sequences, while 629 sequences were considered due to alignment and duplication errors. The copy number of LACS genes were close among all lineages ranging from a single copy detected in Rhodophyta, Chlorophyta, Embryophyte, and Ferns to the nine sequences in order Brassicales and Poales (Table S1). To verify the reliability of BLAST results, InterproScan and Pfam analysis was performed on all protein sequences while most of them were classified into the LACS family. Sequence information is provided in Supplementary Table S2.

3.2. Phylogenetic relationships

Evolutionary relationships among the plants LACS were investigated by reconstructing the phylogenetic tree using protein sequences of A. thaliana and supposed LACS identified by homology searches in 122 species. The phylogenetic analysis of LACS amino acid sequences appeared in a well-resolved tree by ML and Bayesian methods, showing the formation of six main clades with one algal and ferns sequences (Fig. 1). The clade I & II include LACS1 & 2 respectively and some hypothetical sequences. Clade III consists of LACS3-5, while LACS6 and 7 were dominated in clade IV. Subsequently, clade V includes LACS9, and clade VI contains LACS8 (Table S2, Figure S2). Some of the algal LACS sequences revealed a separate outgroup with different LACS. Clade I indicated the most ancient forms as well as a divergence from the land plants.

In all clades, algal, bryophytes, fern (Selaginella moellendorffii), and basal angiosperm (Amborella trichopoda) were phylogenetically divergent from land plants. Within clade I, Kalanchoe laxiflora, Kalanchoe fedtschenkoi, Trifolium pratense, Panicum virgatum contain more than one sequence. Medicago truncatula, Glycine max, Lotus japonicus in clade II contains more than one sequence while in clade III and IV, 27 and 26 species contain more than one sequence, respectively. More than one sequence in many clades possibly presenting gene duplication events.

Clade III was the largest clade consist of 120 sequences of LACS3-5 of all angiosperms with 25, 16, 10, 8 Brassicaceae, Fabaceae, Solanaceae, Cucurbitaceae family genes respectively accounting for 18% of all LACS genes. Clade IV was the 2nd largest clade consists of 115 sequences of LACS6 and 7 having 17 genes from Brassicaceae, 12 genes from Fabaceae, 10 genes from Malvaceae, 8 genes from Euphorbiaceae, and 6 genes from Chlorophyta of three different families (Mamiellaceae, Chlorellaceae, Bathycoccaceae) (Figure S1 and Table S1). Overall, the phylogenetic relationship of LACS genes from all species reveals that LACS genes are broadly distributed in flowering plants especially in order Brassicales the copy number was expanded in most species.

3.3. LACS gene structure and organization analysis

We examined the intron/exon organization for each gene to explore the structural diversity of LACS genes as shown in Fig. 2 (details in Table S3). Our results disclosed intron number of these genes ranged from 5 to 22. Many of the selected LACS retrieved by BLAST searches contain more than seven introns except for Manihot esculenta in clade VI. Among LACS clades, the intron number and gene structure were relatively conserved. The maximum of the LACS from land plants is up to 22 introns, indicating a potential increase of introns in land plant LACS. Species close to each other in the phylogenetic tree show similar intron number. For example, Clade IV had the maximum number of introns except for one species (Medicago truncatula) having ten introns. The maximum number of introns in Clade I-IV were 18, while Clade V and VI had fewer introns ranging from 5 to 10 (Fig. 2). We investigated intron phases across all LACS of representative species and the results showed quite variable intron phase pattern across plant LACS. Phase 2 was dominant across genes with more than one intron. The intron phase 2,0,0,0,1 is significantly conserved across (LACS2-4) that grouped in clades I–III (Fig. 2).

The conserved or consensus sequence in the protein or nucleotide sequence is known as a motif (Magwanga et al., 2018). Conserved motifs and gene structural variation are potential components for the evolution and diversity of the multigene family. We examined the conserved motifs in all studied species by MEME in order to further explore diversification of the LACS gene family. In total, 20 motifs were identified in LACS. Motifs 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, 13, 14, 16 as shown in Fig. 3 were common in all clades. Most of the motifs share all clades, except for motif 19 that was only present in Clade III, V, and VI. Among different amino acids conserved in the 20 motifs, Leucine frequency was the highest (0.0907), followed by Glycine (0.0833), Valine (0.0798), Lysine (0.0726), and Glutamic acid (0.069), etc., while the minimum frequency was 0.0141 for the Tryptophan amino acid. According to InterProScan and GenomeNet annotations, Motifs 1–6, and 8–11 were an AMP-binding enzyme domain. A number of specific amino acids residues within the motifs were associated with AMP-binding signatures, as reported in previous studies, i.e. for motifs 1 and 4 (Watkins and Ellis, 2012), for motifs 2 and 4 by (Weimar et al., 2002), and for motifs 4 and 19 by (Andersson et al., 2012). These motifs are functionally important for LACS proteins, while the remaining conserved motifs were not annotated in InterProScan and GenomeNet yet. All these identified motifs were part of AMP-dependent synthetase which play an important role in lipid biosynthesis and distribution within the cell.

3.4. Evaluation of LACS subcellular localization

Protein properties were evaluated including protein length and their subcellular location (Table S4). The sequence identity of all studied LACS with that of A. thaliana was compared in NCBI. The identified sequences showed similarity with LACS1-9 sequences mostly ranging from 33% (Volvox carteri) to 99.28% (Arabidopsis helleri in Clade V), indicating a close relationship among all LACS genes. The prediction of subcellular locations showed that members of LACS1-5 (Clade 1–3) mostly found in the endoplasmic reticulum and cytoplasm. Our results revealed isoforms belonging to the same clade have different subcellular localization, it may be likely due to large and small-scale changes in the structure and functions of genes among species over evolutionary time. This diversification facilitates an organism to evolve new genes that participate in various pathways occurring in different subcellular localizations (Shockey and Browse, 2011). LACS6 and 7 (Clade IV) were predicted to be localized in plastid and peroxisomes. LACS8 (Clade VI) was mostly reported to locate in the ER and some of them were predicted to locate in the plastid. About 82% of LACS9 (Clade V) were predicted to locate in the plastid. 180 out of 629 sequences were predicted to locate in the ER and 175 were localized in the plastid. The lowest number (6) of sequences were predicted to locate in the cell membrane (Table S4; Fig. 4). As the subcellular localization of most Arabidopsis LACS were experimentally determined, the prediction show in this study with other species may be due to gene duplication events but the mechanism underlying functional divergence are not well understood due to alteration of single amino acid replacement or divergence.
