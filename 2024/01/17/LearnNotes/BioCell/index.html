L16 Protein targeting continued. How are proteins imported into the nucleus?

  • Many nuclear proteins contain a nuclear localization sequence (NLS)
  • Nuclear import factors bind to the NLS.
  • Import factors can ferry the NLS containing cargo across the pore.
  • The nucleoplasm (inner) contains high concentrations of Ran-GTP
  • Ran-GTP binds to the import factor causing a substantial conformational change that releases the NLS from the import factor

How it works: Nuclear transport receptors and a gradient of Ran-GTP drive transport of cargo across the pore.

Summary of understanding of nuclear import ca. 1988

  1. Nuclear pores: Nuclear proteins enter the nucleus through nuclear pores. Same was demonstrated for nuclear export.
  2. Size limitation: Proteins ~40 kilodaltons or less can cross the nuclear pore.
  3. NLS overcomes size limits:Proteins larger than that can only cross the pore if they have an NLS.
  4. NLS: The NLS is necessary and sufficient for localization to the nucleus.

A summary of where we are now.

  • Nuclear transport goes through nuclear pores.
  • Proteins that reside in the nucleus often contain a nuclear localization sequence (NLS) that directs them to the nucleus.
  • Nuclear import also depends on soluble factors present in the cytosol that direct NLS containing proteins to the nuclear pore and drive their translocation/accumulation in the nucleus.
  • One of these cytosolic factors is a small GTPase known as Ran.
  • Ran is not necessary for binding of NLS containing proteins to the nuclear pore, but it is necessary for the NLS cargo translocate/accumulate in the nucleus.
  • Nuclear transport also requires energy in the form of nucleotide triphosphates.

“Importin” is actually a heterodimer

Our cells actually express many other nuclear import factors as well as nuclear export factors that are tasked for moving different cargo across nuclear pores.
Importin alpha/beta was the first discovered. It is responsible for most of the transport involving proteins that contain an NLS.

  • Importin-alpha is an adaptor protein that binds To a subset of NLSs, but it alone cannot enter nuclear pores. However, it can form a complex with importin-beta
  • Importin-beta is the actual “nuclear transporter” or “NTR” that can enter the nuclear pore.

  • You can think of nuclear import as rectified diffusion.

  • Now you know that AP-2 binds to (4,5)P2 but not to (3,4)P2, and you also know the concentration at which (4,5)P2 binding to AP-2 saturates. Next, measure the binding of the LDL-cytoplasmic tail (which is where the internalization signal in LDL resides) to a fixed amount of AP-2 in the presence of a saturating concentration of (4,5)P2 versus an equimolar concentration of (3,4)P2. The result shows that LDL-R only binds AP-2 in the presence of the right phosphoinositide. Hence the statement “an endocytosis signal in the cytoplasmic tail of LDL receptors binds the membrane adaptor AP-2 after its conformation has been locally unlocked by binding to PI(4,5)P2 in the plasma membrane.”

  • Note, you could have also compared binding of AP-2 to LDL-R cytotail in presence of (4,5)P2 versus no added phosphoinositide. That too would work and suffice. It’s just more exciting to see it compared to another phosphoinositide which reveals even greater specificity.
    From here, you could do a number of experiments to better understand the mechanism and physiological significance of this biochemistry. You could, for example, perform a deletion analysis to better define the region in LDL-R that binds to AP-2. You could test the physiological significance of this biochemistry by replacing the WT LDL-R with a mutant version that does not bind to AP-2 and measure the consequences for LDL-R uptake.
