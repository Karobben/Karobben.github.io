L2: Scissors

  • 1980: Organization of genes in the ftsA-envA region of the Escherichia coli genetic map and identification of a new fts locus (ftsZ)

  • 1985: Overproduction of FtsZ induces minicell formation in E. coli

    • ftsZ is more important than ftsQ and ftsA!

  • 1991: FtsZ ring structure associated with division in Escherichia coli

    • Cell Biology: Here we have used immuno electron microscopy to localize the FtsZ protein to the division site. (gold-labelled secondary antibody)

Three paper shown FtzS are GTP binding protein independently

  • 1992: Escherichia coli cell-division gene ftsZ encodes a novel GTP-binding protein

  • 1992: The essential bacterial cell-division protein FtsZ is a GTPase

    • [α32P]GTP illustrate the FtsZ GTP-binding activity

  • 1993: Escherichia coli cell division protein FtsZ is a guanine nucleotide binding protein

    • FtsZ is a GTP/GDP binding protein with GTPase activity (biochemistry approach)
    • [γ32P]GTP illustrate the FtsZ GTP-binding activity

  • 2017: GTPase activity–coupled treadmilling of the bacterial tubulin FtsZ organizes septal cell wall synthesis

    • dynamic treadmilling predominantly determined by its guanosine triphosphatase activity

  • 2017: Treadmilling by FtsZ filaments drives peptidoglycan synthesis and bacterial cell division

    • The mechanism by which bacteria divide is not well understood. Cell division is mediated by filaments of FtsZ and FtsA (FtsAZ) that recruit septal peptidoglycan-synthesizing enzymes to the division site. To understand how these components coordinate to divide cells, we visualized their movements relative to the dynamics of cell wall synthesis during cytokinesis. We found that the division septum was built at discrete sites that moved around the division plane. FtsAZ filaments treadmilled circumferentially around the division ring and drove the motions of the peptidoglycan-synthesizing enzymes. The FtsZ treadmilling rate controlled both the rate of peptidoglycan synthesis and cell division. Thus, FtsZ treadmilling guides the progressive insertion of new cell wall by building increasingly smaller concentric rings of peptidoglycan to divide the cell.

Muscle

  • Development of the first microscope by van Leeuwenhoek (late 1600’s)
  • First microscopic observations of muscles (late 1600’s)
  • 1864:
    • 2 different bands were observed in microscopic muscle tissue using cell biology (Liège, Belgium)
    • “Myosin” was purified from muscle using biochemistry (Leipzig, Germany)
  • 1939: Myosin and adenosinetriphosphatase
    • “Myosin” itself was shown to have ATPase activity(Biochemistry approach)
  • 1941: Biochemistry approach discovered ”myosin” is really actin and myosin, Actin was thought to activate myosin
  • 1942: Das kontraktile Element des Muskels [The contractile element of muscles]
    • Purified threads of actin + myosin
    • Purified threads of actin + myosin shorten dramatically after exposure to ATP!!
    • Even after this result, Szent-Györgyi strongly supported the folding hypothesis
  • 1954: two natrue paper:
    • Paper 1: Changes in the Cross-Striations of Muscle during Contraction and Stretch and their Structural Interpretation
      • Cell biology shows that contraction is due to changes in the size of actin-containing bands.
    • Paper 2: Structural changes in muscle during contraction; interference microscopy of living muscle fibres
      • Cell biology shows that stretch is due to changes in the size of actin-containing bands
    • Above pictures are from paper 1 and 2
  • Two kinds of model:
    • Constrain model: 1942 support the folding
    • Sliding model: Shown to be sliding filaments of actin and myosin
    • Disassemble Model
  • 1957: THE DOUBLE ARRAY OF FILAMENTS IN CROSS-STRIATED MUSCLE
    • Cell biology shows the precise structure of actin and myosin in muscle

  • 1987: Myosin subfragment-1 is sufficient to move actin filaments in vitro
    • ATP-driven changes in S1 Head of myosin

Lecture 7 - How do bacteria move?

  • 1838: Flagella seen
  • 1962-1966: ENOMOTO (1966) measured ATP and ATPase activity in paralyzed mutants of Salmonella. He found that the ATP content and ATPase activity per cell were not significantly different between several paralyzed mutants and the wild type. Isolated flagella of neither wild-type nor paralyzed mutants showed ATPase activity (ENOMOTO 1962).
  • 1966: Genetical Studies of Non-flagellate Mutants of Salmonella
    • Genetic approach identifies genes required for flagella
    • flaA, flaВ, flaC, flaD, flaF, flaJ and flaK
  • 1966: Genetic Studies of Paralyzed Mutants in Salmonella. I. Genetic Fine Structure of the mot Loci in SALMONELLA TYPHIMURIUM
    • Genetic approach identifies motA and motB
  • 1969: Location of Genes for Motility and Chemotaxis on the Escherichia coli Genetic Map
    • Genetic approach identifies genes required for flagella (flaA, flaB, flaC, …)
    • hypothesis: ATP drives the motion of the flagella
  • 1974: Chemomechanical coupling without ATP: the source of energy for motility and chemotaxis in bacteria
    • Add CCCP, an uncoupler of the membrane proton gradient: uncoupler of oxidative phosphorylation, paralyzes bacteria. even though ATP remains present.
    • proton motive force

L8 How does a cell make flagella?

  • 1974: Assembly of Salmonella flagellin in vitro and in vivo
    • Formation of a flagellar filament of Salmonella from its component protein, flagellin, is in principle a self-assembly process, which proceeds by the addition of flagellin monomers one by one to the distal end of the filament.
    • Biochemistry approach: Filament monomers self-assemble
  • 1978: Incomplete flagellar structures in nonflagellate mutants of Salmonella typhimurium
    • The present data indicate that the flagellar morphogenesis proceeds at the bacterial cell en- velope from the inner to the outer portion.
  • 1980: Functional homology of fla genes between Salmonella typhimurium and Escherichia coli
    • SUMMARY: Summary. Genetic studies have shown the presence of more than 20 fla genes indispensable for the forma- tion of flagella in Salmonella typhimurium and Escher- ichia coli.
    • An attempt at consistent naming between Salmonella and E. coli
  • 1988: New unified nomenclature for the flagellar genes of Escherichia coli and Salmonella typhimurium
    • 40 genes

  • 1990: Gene fliA encodes an alternative sigma factor specific for flagellar operons in Salmonella typhimurium
    • Biochemistry approach
  • 1992: A novel transcriptional regulation mechanism in the flagellar regulon of Salmonella typhimurium: an antisigma factor inhibits the activity of the flagellum-specific sigma factor, sigma F
